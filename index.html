Lack of Confidence in Approximate Bayesian Computation Model Choice

Christian P. Robert, Jean-Marie Cornuet, Jean-Michael Marin, and Natesh S. Pillai

Annika Helverson

12/6/22

Motivation

  • Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) is a method used to analyze complex stochastic models particularly when more standard methods like Monte Carlo simulation are intractable
    • Monte Carlo methods are not available if evaluating the likelihood is too computationally expensive
    • Using ABC allows us to avoid evaluating a likelihood
  • ABC is s a valid method for Bayesian inference, but we need justification for model selection procedures

ABC Rejection Algorthim

The most basic ABC framework, ABC rejection algorithm, procedes as follows:

for i = 1:N
    # check if simulated data similar enough to observed data
    while ρ(z,y) > ϵ 
        # generate θ_draw from the prior
        θ_draw = rand(π(.)) 
        # generate z from the likelihood
        z = rand(f(.|θ_draw)) 
    end
    θ[i] = θ_draw
end
  • With increasing dimension, the probability of simulated a dataset, \(z\), being close enough to the observed data, \(y\), decreases

ABC and Summary Statistics

  • Replace \(y\) with summary statistic \(\eta(y)\) to reduce dimension
  • \(\text{If } \eta \text{ is sufficient with respect to } \theta \text{ and } \epsilon \text{ is small enough, then }\)
    • \(\pi_\epsilon(\theta|y) = \int \pi_\epsilon(\theta, z|y)dz \approx \pi(\theta|y)\)
  • If \(\eta\) is not sufficient, \(\pi_\epsilon(\theta|y) \rightarrow \pi(\theta|\eta(y))\) as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\)
  • With sufficient statistics, computational efficiency increases with no information loss
    • Only the \(\textbf{exponential family}\) has sufficient statistics with finite dimension

Bayesian Model Selection

  • In Lecture 5 we discussed Bayes Factors for model comparison
  • Recall that the Bayes factor for comparing two models is: \(B_{12} = \frac{w_1(y)}{w_2(y)} = \frac{\int_{\Theta_1} \pi_i(\theta_1) f_1(y|\theta_1) d \theta_1}{\int_{\Theta_2} \pi_i(\theta_2) f_2(y|\theta_2) d \theta_2}\) where \(w_k(y)\) is the marginal likelihood for model \(k\).
  • Model choice across \(M\) models is driven by the posterior distribution of \(\mathcal{M}\), the model index: \(\mathbb{P}(\mathcal{M} = m|y) = \pi(\mathcal{M}=m) w_m(y) / \sum_k \pi(\mathcal{M} = k) w_k(y)\)
  • Straightforward to interpret but not viable in the “no likelihood” setting (ABC)

ABC Modification for Model Choice (ABC-MC)

  • Modify ABC to incorporate \(\mathcal{M}\) into the parameters lets ABC estimate the posterior probability \(\pi(\mathcal{M} = m|y)\)
for i = 1:N
    # check if simulated data similar enough to observed data
    while ρ(η(z), η(y)) > ϵ 
        # generate m from the prior π(M=m)
        m = rand(π(M=m))
        # generate θ_m from the prior π_m(θ_m)
        θ_m = rand(π_m(θ_m)) 
        # generate z from the model f_m(z|θ_m)
        z = rand(f_m(z|θ_m)) 
    end
    m[i] = m
    θ[i] = θ_m
end

ABC-MC Approximate Bayes Factor

  • Frequency of acceptances from model \(m\) is the ABC estimate of the posterior probability: \(\hat{\pi} \ (\mathcal{M}=m|y) = N^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}_{m^{(i)} = m}\)
  • Yields the following approximation to a Bayes factor: \(\hat{B}_{12}(y) = \frac{\pi(\mathcal{M}=2) \sum_{t=1}^T \mathbb{1}_{m^t = 1} \mathbb{1}_{\rho\{ \eta(z^t), \eta(y) \} \leq \epsilon}}{\pi(\mathcal{M}=1) \sum_{t=1}^T \mathbb{1}_{m^t = 2} \mathbb{1}_{\rho\{ \eta(z^t), \eta(y) \} \leq \epsilon}}\)
    • \(T\) is the number of simulations required to achieve \(N\) accepted datasets

Approximation \(\hat{B}_{12}\) in the Limit

Consider \(T \rightarrow \infty\) and a uniform prior on the model index. Then the limit of \(\hat{B}_{12}(y)\) is:

\[\begin{align} B^{\epsilon}_{12}(y) &= \frac{\mathbb{P} [ \mathcal{M} = 1, \rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon ]}{\mathbb{P} [ \mathcal{M} = 2, \rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon ]} \\ &= \frac{\int \mathbb{1}_{\rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon} \pi_1(\theta_1) f_1(z|\theta_1) dz d\theta_1}{\int \mathbb{1}_{\rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon} \pi_2(\theta_2) f_2(z|\theta_2) dz d\theta_2} \\ &= \frac{\int \mathbb{1}_{\rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon} \pi_1(\theta_1) f_1^{\eta}(\eta|\theta_1) d \eta d\theta_1}{\int \mathbb{1}_{\rho \{ \eta(z), \eta(y)\} \leq \epsilon} \pi_2(\theta_2) f_2^{\eta}(\eta|\theta_2) d \eta d\theta_2} \end{align}\]

Approximation \(\hat{B}_{12}\) in the Limit

  • \(f_1^{\eta}(\eta | \theta_1)\) and \(f_2^{\eta}(\eta | \theta_2)\) denote the densities of \(\eta(z)\) for \(z \sim f_1(z|\theta_1)\) and \(z \sim f_2(z|\theta_2)\) respectively

Thus, \(B_{12}^{\eta}(y) \rightarrow \frac{\int \pi_1(\theta_1) f_1^{\eta}(\eta(y) | \theta_1) d \theta_1}{\int \pi_2(\theta_2) f_2^{\eta}(\eta(y) | \theta_2) d \theta_2}\) as \(\epsilon \rightarrow 0\)

  • When \(\epsilon = 0\), ABC inference only uses information from summary statistic \(\eta(y)\)

Insufficient Summary Statistics

  • ABC still converges under standard regularity conditions (Bernardo and Smith, 1994)
    • Insuffient summary statistics are valid if your goal is \(\textbf{point estimation}\) (Fearnhead and Prangle, 2012)
  • However, if your goal is \(\textbf{model selection}\), loss of information from using \(\eta(y) \text{ instead of } y\) can impact model choice

Insufficient \(\eta(y)\)

Even if \(\eta(y)\) is sufficient for both \(f_1(t|\theta_1) \text{ and } f_2(t|\theta_2)\) it \(\textit{does not}\) imply sufficiency for \(\{ mf_m(y|\theta_m)\}\) :

\[\begin{align} B_{12}(y) &= w_1(y) / w_2(y) \\ &= \frac{\int_{\Theta_1} \pi(\theta_1) g_1(y) f_1^{\eta}(\eta(y)| \theta_1) d \theta_1}{\int_{\Theta_2} \pi(\theta_2) g_2(y) f_2^{\eta}(\eta(y)| \theta_2) d \theta_2} \\ &= \frac{g_1(y) \int \pi_1(\theta_1) f_1^{\eta}(\eta(y) | \theta_1) d \theta_1}{g_2(y) \int \pi_2(\theta_2) f_2^{\eta}(\eta(y) | \theta_2) d \theta_2} \\ B_{12}(y) &= \frac{g_1(y)}{g_2(y)} B_{12}^{\eta}(y) \end{align}\]

Insufficient \(\eta(y)\)

We just showed that in the best case, \(B_{12}(y) = \frac{g_1(y)}{g_2(y)} B_{12}^{\eta}(y)\)

  • \(g_1(y) = g_2(y)\) in special cases (i.e. Gibbs random field)
  • Implies modelwise sufficient statistics are typically not sufficient across models
  • Thus, the ABC-MC approximation shown earlier will not always converge to the true Bayes factor
  • In fact, as \(n\) grows, \(g_1(y) / g_2(y)\) can be arbitrarily small or arbitrarily large

Example: Modelwise Sufficient Statistic

Consider a sample, \(y = (y_1, \dots, y_n)\) that might be from a Poisson \(\mathcal{P}(\lambda)\) distribution or a geometric \(\mathcal{G}(p)\) distribution

  • For both models (but not across models), \(S = \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i = \eta(y)\) is a sufficient statistic
  • Under the Poisson model, the sample conditioned on the sufficient statistic has a multinomial distribution: \(y|S \sim \mathcal{M}(S, 1/n, \dots, 1/n)\)
  • Under the Geometric model, the conditional distribution is instead Uniform

Example: Modelwise Sufficient Statistic

The discrepancy ratio is \(g_1(y) / g_2(y) = {n + S - 1 \choose S} S! \ n^{-S} / \prod_i y_i!\)

  • Since the likelihood is known and tractable, we can compare the exact Bayes factor to the ABC-MC estimate to reveal the discrepancy
  • We assign priors to the Poisson and Geometric models respectively with \(\lambda \sim Exp(1)\) and \(p \sim Unif(0, 1)\)
  • Conduct ABC-MC and compute both the true Bayes factor, \(B_{12}(y)\) and the estimated Bayes factor, \(B^{\eta}_{12}(y)\)

Discussion

  • Some imprecision is expected with ABC
  • For model selection, the magnitude of the imprecision can be arbitrarily small or arbitrarily large which could lead to erroneous conclusions
    • Need information on \(g_1(y) / g_2(y)\) which is typically not available when ABC is required
  • Model selection for ABC requires computationally expensive simulation-based methods or a summary statistic that is sufficient across models
